It's easy to find your purpose again after retirement

By Alice Adams

It's easy to find your purpose again after retirement
1  /  1
Back to Gallery

Because of better health in later years and longer lifespans, many retirees find themselves in the "been there, done that, now what?" cycle. It's a phenomenon that occurs after traveling to all those places you wanted to visit, getting in all those golf games or hunting trips or it may be having the time to drink your coffee and complete the crossword puzzle each morning.

Of course, there are some who reach retirement age and keep working, either because they need the money or because they like what they do and aren't ready to quit.

But, there are those who haven't found retirement's "sweet spot," their comfort zone in a life with a lot less structure and/or demand for their contributions. So, what's next? Where do they find a reason to get up every morning?

If we take a look to how our parents and grandparents handled it, we may find that neither of these generations lived long enough after retirement to get bored, or were in such poor health, they didn't have much of a chance to enjoy retirement.

In many ways, the baby boomer generation - and, with any luck, their parents - are pioneers in learning how to fill their retirement years with activities they are passionate about.

It may also surprise you many 75-plus Houstonians are continuing to work 40-plus hours a week and enjoying every minute. Teachers continue to teach, especially in disciplines in which there are shortages - such as nursing and some of the STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics) disciplines. Engineers continue to solve problems and many with business, sales and management expertise continue in consulting roles.

If you're looking for purpose, think about everything you've done in your life, beginning with your first jobs in your teen years. Then, ask yourself these questions:

What have I done that didn't seem like work?

What have I done that I've never grown tired of doing?

What have I done that energized me, either intellectually or emotionally?

The answer to these three questions will lead you to your passion.

A resident in one of Houston's senior communities had enjoyed carpentry much of his life and spent his free time building projects, large and small. Another retiree said he enjoyed working in a mentoring program in the schools in his community. One retiree volunteered with Meals on Wheels for years until her arthritis took the upper hand. Another volunteered to read books, recording them for the blind … and another worked in a hospital volunteer program three days a week.

Almost every senior center has a dance performance group. If you think you might enjoy tap dancing, line dancing or clogging, now's your chance to dance up a storm.

One of my favorite retirees, the late Roberta Keys Torn, was a graduate of Baylor University (along with her three sisters - they were quadruplets), reared her three children with her husband Rip and enjoyed being a Houston homemaker. But after her kids were up and gone, and when she took a friend to M.D. Anderson for cancer treatment, Roberta noticed several patients going through their treatments alone.

That's when she helped found and work for Adopt-a-Friend, making trips to the Cancer Center several days a week and advocating for cancer patients (she arranged for laundry facilities for long-term patients and low-cost apartments for out-of-town patients).

This energetic retiree, who lived well into her 90s, also was a friendly face, a caring touch, and a visitor and friend for M.D. Anderson patients without family nearby … and for those who failed to defeat cancer, back three or four decades ago, Roberta traveled to be with the families of her "adopted friends" at their funerals.

So, whether your retirement passion ends up being some form of mentoring, higher learning, traveling, gardening, or helping others, you'll probably have to make some sacrifices to make it a regular part of your life. This sacrifice could be as simple as taking time away from TV or something more impactful like time away from family and friends.

But here's the trick. Don't expect your passion or mission in retirement will just plop into your lap. Instead, be willing to put yourself into situations that expose you to new and different environments, offering new opportunities. Try out different things.

If visiting hospitals or nursing homes aren't your thing, try a class in something totally off the wall. A 90-year-old friend attended a poetry-writing class at his senior community and ended up writing enough good poetry to publish a book. Another friend gives talks around town about senior safety and still another, in her 70s, started a new business with her son.

My humble advice: Turn over lots of rocks. Don't count out anything. Try various classes and crafts. Ever heard of Grandma Moses, the painter? She didn't create her first folk art masterpiece until she was in her 70s. Use your old skills or develop new ones … but follow your passion. You'll be glad you did.

6 ways to find purpose in retirement

Retirement not what you thought it would be? Find yourself missing the structure of going to the job every day? Try these tips to add meaning to your retirement:

1. Don't consider yourself retired. Instead, think of yourself as pursuing other options. This is a new beginning in your life.

2. Get to know people with diverse interests. Join Toastmasters or a men's or women's group. Don't hesitate to get to know new people as you build existing relationships.

3. Maintain your health. Continue with your regular annual health checkups. Join the YMCA. Look into improving your overall well-being through swimming, walking, yoga or meditation. The better you feel, the more enjoyable retirement will be.

4. Join a worthwhile cause. Not only will reaching out to the less fortunate improve your self-esteem but you will make the world a better place, enriching lives, including your own.

5. Learning is a lifelong process, so take time to take that class you've always wanted to take.

6. Think young. Be energetic and enjoy the time you now have on your hands. Retirement doesn't mean giving up everything. Age is a number, and attitude is everything.
