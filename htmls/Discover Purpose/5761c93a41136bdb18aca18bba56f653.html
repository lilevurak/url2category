'My purpose ... to just help one person'

The hardest thing Nita Grizzard thought she would ever face was losing her mother to breast cancer.

Grizzard was 17 years old, a senior in high school, and losing her mother was losing her best friend. A woman she describes as determined and strong. A mom who cut the crust off her sandwiches and a woman who left her the strength she needed to get through her own eventual battles.

Like her own breast cancer diagnosis this year, cervical cancer in 2012, losing twin daughters shortly after they entered the world 10 years ago, and a daughter's brain tumor in 2002.

Grizzard was only 11 years old when her mother, Joan Neal, was diagnosed with breast cancer at the age of 34. For six years, the young daughter accompanied her mother to chemo and radiation treatments, and spent countless nights with her in the hospital. Neal died in January 1991 at the age of 40.

"I cant tell you how that changed my life," said Grizzard, now 43 years old. "Having to grow up without my mom to see me graduate, to see me get married, to be with me when I had my beautiful daughters, to share in the happy times and even more in the heartaches. Its been 25 years and it doesnt get any easier. I miss her every day of my life."

Today, Grizzard is the mother to four children: Katie, 24; Abbie, 19; and her twin daughters. Born at 25 weeks old on Aug. 14, 2006, Lucie lived for eight hours, and Lillie, for eight days.

"I have prayed God would let this cancer end with me," she said. "I don't want to pass it on to my girls. I'm not afraid of dying, because I know I'll go to heaven. I'm saved. It's the fear of leaving my kids behind because I know what it feels like."

'If I could just make it to 40'

A cross country and track runner in high school, Grizzard was training for her first half marathon in June 2012 when she began having problems with her menstrual cycle. Although she thought it was because she was running so much, something urged her to call her doctor.

Tests revealed she had Stage 1B Adenocarcinoma of the Endocervix  or cervical cancer.

"I remember my husband, David, and I walking out of that office and going to sit in the courtyard of the hospital," Grizzard said. "We just sat there and cried. We were in shock."

Grizzard's husband, who is the CFO of Baptist Medical Center East, said it is "quite impressive to see and watch and to know she's doing all she can to make it. You don't realize what someone goes through with cancer ... unless you're there and living it.

"She is the strongest person I have ever known. Mentally and emotionally and spiritually. She's been such a witness and perseveres and continues to fight. She's just such a fighter. She was very competitive in high school, and always wanted to be the best that she could be. She's one of those people through her life ... she deals with it and continues to move on."

Grizzard said she always thought, "If I could just make it to 40, I'll be all right. Well, at almost 39, I was diagnosed with cervical cancer. For a long time, I thought, 'This is it. It's going to be just like my mom. I'm going to die. I'm not going to be here for my girls. I'm not going to be here for my husband.'"

While she did not undergo chemotherapy or radiation treatments, Grizzard said it took six weeks to recover fully. And while her body recovered, her mind did not.

"I was living in constant fear," she said. "Every ache, every pain, I worried that the cancer had returned. It was consuming me and stealing my joy, my happiness, taking away the life that I had been given."

Following an oncology check-up in January 2013, doctors became worried about some lymph nodes that had appeared in Grizzard's groin area on her right side. They wanted to remove her left ovary that had not been removed previously because it continued to have cysts every month. Following surgery, they learned the cancer had not returned.

And it was then Grizzard decided to stop living in fear.

Nita Grizzard was the keynote speaker at the Making Strides Against Breast Cancer event this year. Grizzard is a two-time cancer survivor.  (Photo: Contributed)

"I had to take control of this fear before it completely destroyed me," she said.

So she started running again, and more than two months after her last surgery, ran the Finish on the 50 Half Marathon in Auburn. She has since raced in the Disney Princess Half Marathon and the Montgomery Half Marathon.

"Crossing that finish line was like kicking cancer in the face and showing cancer that I had control," she said of the Auburn race. "I was not going to let cancer have a hold on me any longer. I wont lie and say that I never let it worry me, especially every six months when I go for an oncology check-up. Im human and I let that fear start creeping back in until I hear the doctor say all is fine."

One in three women

More than two years after that check up, in October 2015, Grizzard felt a spot on her left breast during a self breast exam. Following an ultrasound, she was told there was nothing to worry about.

In February, she received a reminder in the mail for her annual mammogram and breast MRI. She instinctively felt for the lump again and not only was it still there, it was larger.

"I have always thought in the back of my mind, am I going to be the one?" Grizzard said. "One in three women are diagnosed with breast cancer. And, back in 2008, I did the BRCA 1 and 2 (gene test), and it came back negative on one and unspecified on another."

'Designing Woman' paints to heal

Grizzard became the "one in three" when she was later diagnosed after repeatedly being told "it was nothing serious" following mammograms, ultrasounds, biopsies  with invasive ductal carcinoma.

"I guess somewhere deep in my heart I knew that was going to be the words that I was told," she said. "One of the worst parts now was having to tell my husband, my children and my family that I have cancer again. Before I told anyone, I went to my bedroom and took about 15 minutes to cry. I sat down in my bedroom floor and I cried out to The Lord to heal me, please spare my life once again."

And to be with her husband and children when she told them. To give them peace and understanding.

"I also thanked Him for being that voice in my head to urge me to call the doctor, to urge me to pursue things further even when everyone was saying it was nothing to worry about," she said. "I thanked Him for being my healer and my comforter. He had healed me before, I knew that He could and would do it again."

She had a double mastectomy on May 11, with immediate reconstruction and removal of lymph nodes. And with her oncotype score having come back in the intermediate range, she did not undergo chemo or radiation.

Living through storms

"The week before I had my mammogram and MRI, we were at church and the pastor was talking about 'storms' that people go through in their lives," Grizzard said. "He said everyone is either going through a storm, has just gone through a storm or was gearing up for a storm. I remember sitting there and thanking God that we had already gone through so many storms in our life, thanking Him for getting us through them and that we were on the other side of those storms."

That was after her cervical cancer. It was after losing her twins after they entered the world at 25 weeks and weighing less than 2 pounds each. It was after her then 5-year-old daughter Abbie was diagnosed with a brain tumor on her pituitary gland.

Grizzard remembers praying about Abbie, "God, please don't take my baby from me. Don't let me lose my daughter like I did my mom. Im not strong enough to endure that pain again.

Today, while she continually takes medication to control the damage to her pituitary gland from the tumor, surgery and radiation, Abbie is a sophomore at Auburn Montgomery pursuing a nursing degree.

A friend's support, loving her daughters

Leslie Hodges has seen that since she met Nita Grizzard in the third grade when they lived in Rainbow City.

"Nita has always been self-sacrificing, that this is the only time she has put herself first in some regard," she said. "We need to put ourselves first sometimes as women, and I was proud of her for that. She has taught me that a little bit, too. You need to worry about your own health, and you're not going to be good to anyone if you don't care for yourself."

Nita Grizzard, right, with her daughters Abbie, left, and Katie Grizzard Blight.  (Photo: Contributed)

Grizzard's loss of her mother, Hodges said, "just left indelible mark on everybody who experienced that. She's been through so many trials in her life. She was the one in the family who nursed her mother through her illness. We would go to school and she would go home and take care of her mother, would go to the hospital. She would do anything in the world for everybody."

Losing her mother so early taught Grizzard to always show her family how much she loves them.

"I want to give them enough love that if something were to happen to me, that they'll have enough love, that I've given them enough love, to carry them through life," she said. "God has allowed me to go through these storms to help somebody else. My purpose may be to just help one person, or be a sounding board for somebody."

By the numbers

40,450: the approximate number of women in the U.S. who are expected to die from breast cancer in 2016.

2,600: the approximate number of new cases of invasive breast cancer expected to be diagnosed in men in 2016. A mans lifetime risk of breast cancer is about 1 in 1,000.

1 in 8: the number of U.S. women (about 12 percent) who will develop invasive breast cancer over the course of her lifetime.

246,660: the estimated number of new cases of invasive breast cancer expected to be diagnosed in women in the U.S., along with 61,000 new cases of non-invasive (in situ) breast cancer.

2.8 million: the number of women with a history of breast cancer in the U.S., including those undergoing treatment and those who have finished treatment.

Source: breastcancer.org
