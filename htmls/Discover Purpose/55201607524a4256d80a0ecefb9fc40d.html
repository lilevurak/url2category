    In College, Finding Moral Purpose

    SEPT. 16, 2014

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    To the Editor:

    In “Becoming a Real Person” (column, Sept. 9), David Brooks reflects on the responsibility of universities to reimagine what education truly seeks to accomplish.

     The problem is that for far too many, the notion of “higher education” simply suggests the process of amassing lots of facts, acquiring technical skill or methodological jargon and steeping oneself in academic erudition. But academic excellence, however critically important to the process, serves as only half of the equation.

     Higher education must also provide values throughout the academic process. On a curricular level, more courses should seek to engage with the moral dimension of the subject at hand. 

    Universities should provide students with opportunities to engage in meaningful service learning beyond the walls of the university, using their knowledge not only to advance themselves but others as well, and broaden their horizons in the process. They should seek to foster a sense of community, bound not merely by a shared desire for revelry but also by higher notions of responsibility.

     Universities must not be just about making a living but also about building a life. 

    RICHARD M. JOEL
    President, Yeshiva University
    New York, Sept. 9, 2014

    To the Editor:

    David Brooks helpfully clarifies the debate over the purpose of a university education. It is indeed true that our elite institutions, while adept at advancing commercial and cognitive purposes, relegate moral purpose to the realm of haphazard individual decision-making. This splintering-off of the moral purpose of education is one of the chief defects of our otherwise elite educational system.

    Fortunately, there are still many American colleges and universities that embrace what William Deresiewicz, the author of “Excellent Sheep: The Miseducation of the American Elite and the Way to a Meaningful Life,” terms “education, in the highest sense of the word.” Call us old-fashioned, but we who teach at these places see the commercial, cognitive and moral purposes of education as inseparable.

    Our students are just as concerned as Ivy Leaguers about finding a career after graduation, and in our classes we push them to the edge of their cognitive abilities. But we also challenge them, throughout the curriculum and in co-curricular activities, with big questions regarding one’s calling and duties to others. We raise excellent leaders here, not “excellent sheep.”

    JOEL A. JOHNSON
    Sioux Falls, S.D., Sept. 9, 2014

    The writer is an associate professor of government at Augustana College, of the Evangelical Lutheran Church in America.

    To the Editor:

    David Brooks is right to note the vanishing significance of moral values in higher education. And he is right to lay part of the blame on universities, though they are not the entire story.

    Advertisement

    Continue reading the main story

    In the face of increasing tuition, many students and their parents quite understandably seek opportunities that promise lucrative compensation for invested time and effort, and in the process they dismiss, even demean, the ethical concerns of which Mr. Brooks speaks. And in this they mirror society at large.

    I teach at an institution that proudly and consistently emphasizes the moral core of its education. For this, the popular press sanctimoniously questions its motives and gleefully celebrates its foibles (“The Gold and Blue Loses a Bit of Its Luster,” Sports, Aug. 20).

    T. W. MACHAN
    South Bend, Ind., Sept. 9, 2014

    The writer is a professor of English at the University of Notre Dame.
