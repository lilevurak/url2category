Originally Answered: What is the best way to get out of debt?

Generally speaking, the best way to accomplish any goal is first to make the decision to accomplish it and commit to that decision (sounds simple, but it's where most people get tripped up), then to have a plan. Getting out of debt is no different.

Without knowing much about your situation, I can't give specific advice but I can tell you that setting up a budget is essential to any get-out-of-debt plan.

1.  List all of your monthly expenses - everything. It should be easy enough to get your fixed expenses down, but for anything that varies, if you're not sure what  you're currently spending, you will need to track your spending for a month or so to get an idea of what your baseline is. I've used Mint in the past, but have found that Dave Ramsey's EveryDollar http://www.everydollar.com tool works much better for me because it forces the user to take a more active role in categorizing spending. It's my belief that you really should be fully engaged in any area of your life you're trying to improve.

2.  Once you've gotten your spending outlined, it's time to see where you can make some cuts. I'm not going to tell you to cut your cable or to stop those daily Starbucks runs, but what it really comes down to is this: How badly do you want to be debt-free?

You'll need to pull extra cash from wherever in the budget you're able to.

3.  Consider taking on a second job or starting a side business. For most people, the income side of the equation is where you can make the greatest impact.

4.  List your debts smallest to largest in amount, regardless of interest rate.

5.  Blast away at the smallest debt first, hitting it with as much force as you can muster up and paying minimums on all the rest of your debts. When that smallest debt is gone, apply that monthly payment to the next smallest and so forth. You'll do this until you finally hit the largest debt.

A lot of people will disagree with this method because if you are looking at it purely from a mathematical standpoint, paying the debts with the largest interest rates down first makes more sense.  The problem is that debt is not a purely mathematical condition for most people. Emotion plays a much bigger role in the accumulation (and reduction) of debt than we might realize.

So, when you start slashing away at those small debts, you begin to build momentum, pick up steam, etc. and the intensity that you feel toward destroying your debt grows with each of the small "wins" you achieve. You begin to feel like becoming debt-free is a real possibility. You become unstoppable.  This feeling is worth its weight in gold.

The bigger debts start to feel less daunting and before you know it...you're done!

And usually in the time that most people are able to work their way through the "debt snowball", with intensity on their side, the interest that might have been saved by tackling the highest interest debts first turns out to be negligible.

However you decide to tackle your debt, just commit to it with 100% of your being. Feel the restrictions that debt puts on your life, how it limits your options, keeps you in jobs you don't like, and robs you of your future.

If you're truly committed to taking action and can stay connected with the reasons you want to break free of debt, you will get there.

Best of luck to you!

The oversimplified model for paying off debt is:

  1. Spend less
  2. Earn more
  3. Put available cash towards debt

Of course, we all know that paying off debt isnt that simple.

The truth is, to pay off debt (in any reasonable amount of time), you have to deal with a number of more complicated factors: motivation, energy, support from others, income options, short-term goals, and your happiness.

Trying to figure out how to deal with all of those things while youre dealing with the emotional tax of having debt is too much for most people.

Thats why I created a series of straight-forward steps that you can follow in order.

NOTE: Some of the steps might be uncomfortable. If theyre too intense, get creative.

STEPS TO PAY OFF DEBT WITH HIGH MOTIVATION & ENERGY:

#1. ACCEPT THE DEBT CHALLENGE.

To increase your level of motivation and really take *ownership* over your debt situation, I recommend you formally (write it down) accept the challenge of debt. This will give you a sense of ownership and purpose, and will push you forward.

#2. FIND YOUR DEBT FREEDOM PURPOSE

Why do you want to be debt free? Figure this out and youll unleash huge motivation. If you can find a motivator that you *truly* care about, itll get you energized to pay off debt.

#3. GET A QUICK WIN

Whats something you can do that is A) good for you, and B) just challenging enough to make it exciting? If you get a quick win under your belt (e.g., track ALL of your spending for 7 days) youll prove to yourself that you are capable of changing your life.

#4. MAKE A PUBLIC COMMITMENT

This one can be daunting, but its powerful. Consider posting a commitment (towards paying off debt) on Facebook. Or if you prefer, email people. Or if thats too much still, simply tell *one other person* about your commitment. By making a commitment that A) you really care about and value, and B) at least one other person knows about, youll get support while also pushing yourself to take action (because you dont want to have to tell people you failed).

Again if this is too scary, just tell a trusted friend or family member

#5. CREATE A DEBT TRACKER

Grab a piece of paper (or Excel / Google Spreadsheet) and list out all of your debts.

  • Name of debt
  • Balance (how much you owe)
  • Interest rate (or APR: annual percentage rate)
  • Contact details / login (if you want to be hyper organized)

How much debt do you have in total?

#6. CREATE A SPENDING PLAN

PART 1 - CASH FLOW

Again with a piece of paper or spreadsheet, list out the following:

  • All of your monthly expenses (groceries, rent, utilities, phone, internet, etc.)
  • All of your income (full-time job, child support, side job, etc.)

Now compare the two. How much money do you have left over after expenses? If you have negative money left over, then you need to reduce expenses or increase income (coming in steps 8 and 9). If you have cash left over, all of that should be going towards debt if possible.

PART 2 - TRACKING

Grab Mint.com, YNAB (You Need a Budget), GoodBudget, or another tool to track your spending on a day-to-day basis.

Set up spending categories like Food and Rent and allocate a portion of your income each month towards that.

This will help you see your spending in real time and be more realistic with how much you can cut back or how much you can put towards debt.

#7. CHOOSE A DEBT ELIMINATION SYSTEM

OPTION 1 - Debt Snowball (goal is to pay off debt while staying motivated)

  • Order your debts by lowest balance first (credit card A - $1,000, CC B - $2,000, etc.)
  • Pay the minimum payments on all debts
  • Any left over money available goes towards the first (lowest balance) debt
  • After that debt is paid off, move on to the next one

OPTION 2 - Debt Avalanche (goal is to pay off debt as fast as possible)

  • Order your debts by highest interest rate (credit card A - 20%, CC B - 17%, etc.)
  • Pay the minimum payments on all debts
  • Any left over money available goes towards the first (highest interest rate) debt
  • After that debt is paid off, move on to the next one

#8. PRIORITIZE YOUR EXPENSES

Which of your expenses makes you the happiest? Which ones do you need to feel that you are living a good life? Which ones could you do without?

The goal of prioritizing your expenses is to figure out the handful of purchase you make each month (e.g., paying for internet, paying for food, paying for rent well, hopefully not in that order) that make you happy and put them first.

Whatever you spend money on that DOES NOT bring you significant fulfillment and happiness should be drastically reduced or eliminated.

In my case, because I didnt care about where I slept at night, I ditched my apartment altogether and slept in the woods for four months (maybe a bit crazy, but it saved a ton of money).

#9. INCREASE YOUR INCOME

Now that your spending is reduced and you have your money under control, its time to boost your income.

There are far too many options for earning more money than there is space available to list them, but here are some high bang for buck options:

  • Negotiate a raise at work (check Salary.com to see if youre under-earning)
  • Find a new job that pays more
  • Get a side job ( look on Upwork, the world's largest online workplace or RemoteOK.io)
  • Sell your crap (you can always buy it back later)
  • Do odd jobs on Craigslists Gigs section

And thats it. Stay on track with these steps until youve paid off your debt.

I hope these help you. When I had $25K in credit card debt, I was miserable, so if youre hurting right now or feeling depressed or unmotivated, I totally get it.

Thats why these steps pay so much attention to motivation + getting momentum going.
