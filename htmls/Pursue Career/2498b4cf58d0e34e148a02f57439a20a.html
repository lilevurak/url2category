In the 20th century, physicians had the best careers.

In the 21st century, software engineering have the best careers.



Like medicine, software engineering:
  1. involves challenging, creative work
  2. is highly compensated
  3. is changing rapidly enough to stay interesting
Unlike medicine, software engineers can harness the powers of abstraction and automation to have an outsized impact. A physician can only help a few patients a day, but a software engineer can build systems that serve millions of users.

Even if you're mid-career, it's not too late to learn software engineering and have an amazing 21st century career.


Note that the person who asked this question is already working as a software engineer. I can say, having worked in both management and software engineering, that the grass always looks greener on the other side. You have a job, a visa, and a skill set that so many people are working hard to obtain. You may need to change employers, but I'm confident you can parlay all this into an exciting career.

I'm 19 years old.

Just like how you find working as a web developer boring, I found university boring.

I asked the following questions to myself.

"Why is a professor teaching me business calculus when Microsoft Excel is a keyboard and mouse away?"

"Why am I in an environment where my peers have no idea what the f*ck they want to do?"

"Why am I listening to my liberal political science professor hating on Trump?"

So I dropped out and left.

To be honest, I was more afraid to graduate from college without any practical skill than I was to drop out of school.

I saw what my friends were pigeonholing themselves into.

Crazy college loans.

Being around peers who had no idea what their goals were.

Late night partying.

Drugs.

Learning a bunch of nonsense.

I wanted more than a degree that said I was roughly as good as these people who are not focused.

I quickly realized that getting a college degree is not a prerequisite to adding value to a company. Mutually Exclusive.

What am I doing now?

I'm learning how to do growth marketing on my own.

Im going on coffee meetings with people at hot startups, handling growth for Breaking into Startups, and taking various courses on Udemy.

Growth is a career path that college will take 100 years to finally teach due to their bureaucracy.

I would not let not having enough experience" deter you from pivoting to a different career.

I just want to let you know that everyone starts from 0. You're still young. At the age of 24 you still have a super low opportunity cost. I would offer to work for free for people you admire on the side of your day job.

Naval Ravikant, founder of Angellist once tweeted:

"Free education is abundant, all over the Internet. It's the desire to learn that's scarce."

Ysiad Ferreiras was previously a software engineer at a hot startup. He is now VP of sales at an exponentially growing seed-stage startup called Hustle. When he transitioned from developer to his first sales role, he did not care that he did not have 5+ years experience, he just hustled in.

If you want to learn more about Ysiad Ferreiras and how he went from software engineer to VP of sales, definitely checkout this episode of Breaking into Startups.

I have to disagree with Quincy here. Here's why.

A physician is a great career to have. Same goes to software engineers.

Yes, software engineers have the power to change millions of lives just by themselves but that doesn't mean you should pursue that career.

Not everyone is suited for a career as a physician or a software engineer.

I don't know about you but I have tons of experiences from friends where their parents ask them to go be a doctor, an engineer, a lawyer, etc without even considering the fact that their child might not be suitable for these careers.

They are recommending what their child should be based on the pay and the job opportunities.

That is not wrong. However they are missing the very important piece which is "Are you suited to be in this line of field?"

How to pick a career path the right way

Step 1: Find your personality

You have to find what your personality is first. I like to use the Holland Code model.

Basically this model breaks down our major personality types into 6 "codes".

  • R - You like work activities that include practical, hands-on problems and solutions.
  • I - You like working with ideas that require an extensive amount of thinking.
  • A - You like working with designs and patterns.
  • S - You like working with, communicating with and teaching people.
  • E - You like starting up and carrying out projects.
  • C - You like following set procedures and routines.

You can read more about it here: Careers By Interests

Step 2: Find jobs that suit your personality

This is easier said than done because finding your personality isn't easy.

I`ll use myself as an example.

By using the Holland code above, my personality is an ICR.

  • I like to solve problems mentally.
  • I like practical work.
  • I like working with data and details.

What I am not is:

  • I dislike working with art and design where anything can come true. I prefer to work on something that isn't abstract hence my C personality.
  • I prefer to work on my own than with other people.

Here are some of the jobs that fit my personality:

  1. Economist
  2. Mathematician
  3. Network architect
  4. Business analyst
  5. Software developer

Bottom line: Find your personality then find a career that suits your personality.

There are a lot of careers out there that fit different personalities.

Some people think Artistic people can't find jobs. Some people think Hands-on people can't find jobs. These people just didn't do their research. Don't be like them.
