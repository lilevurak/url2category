Essential Skills

Destinations gives students the opportunity to develop essential skills by taking part in both school and work-based experiences.

Essential skills include reading, writing, oral communication, use of documents, use of computers, money math, scheduling, working with others, budgeting and accounting, measurement and calculation, data analysis, estimation, job-task planning, decision making, problem solving, thinking skills, and finding information.

Essential skills are the foundation for learning more sophisticated skills, and are used in almost every occupation and throughout the day in a variety of ways.

Human Resources and Skills Development Canada describes essential skills as follows:

Reading
  • refers to reading material that is in the form of sentences or paragraphs
  • generally involves reading notes, letters, memos, manuals, specifications, regulations, books, reports or journals
  • includes forms and labels if they contain at least one paragraph; print and non print media (for example, texts on computer screens and microfiche); paragraph length text in charts, tables and graphs
Writing
  • includes writing texts and writing in documents (for example, filling in forms), and non paper based writing (for example, typing on a computer)
Oral Communication
  • pertains primarily to the use of speech to give and exchange thoughts and information by workers in an occupational group
Use of Documents
  • refers to tasks that involve a variety of information displays in which words, numbers, icons and other visual characteristics (eg., line, colour, shape) are given meaning by their spatial arrangement i.e. graphs, lists, tables, blueprints, schematics, drawings, signs and labels are documents used in the world of work
Use of Computers
  • indicates the variety and complexity of computer use within the occupational group
Money Math, Scheduling, Budgeting & Accounting, Measurement & Calculation, and Estimation
  • refers to the workers' use of numbers and being required to think in quantitative terms
Working with Others
  • refers to the extent to which employees work with others to carry out their tasks i.e do they have to work co operatively with others; do they have to have the self discipline to meet work targets while working alone
Data Analysis, Job-Task Planning, Decision Making, Problem Solving, Finding Information
  • differentiates between the five different types of thinking skills 


Industry-Specific Skills
In addition to providing students with a solid foundation of workplace essential skills, Destinations also gives students the chance to develop a skill set based on their chosen career pathway.

Depending on the job sector they are interested in, students will develop industry-specific skills through various Destinations programs such as cooperative education or work internship. 

Every industry has a unique skill set required to perform the job duties. These skill sets vary from sector to sector, and can involve anything from the operation of sophisticated machinery to the details of automotive repair.

Life Skills
Life skills are positive behaviors that allow students to deal with the demands and challenges of everyday life. Life skills also enhance psychological and social development such as decision making and problem solving, creative and critical thinking, communication and interpersonal relations, self awareness, and coping with emotions and causes of stress. 

Destinations provides students with experiences that allow them to develop their life skills to their full potential as they mature into caring, lifelong learners.

Technical Skills
Students develop technical skills through workplace experiences in sectors that involve the use of specialized equipment as part of the regular job duties. Everything from computers to agricultural machinery requires the technical expertise of the worker to carry out his or her task. Technical skills are an important part of many jobs in the information age.

Employability Skills
Employability skills are all the critical skills you need in the workplace whether you own your own business or are working with others. Some employability skills include communication, teamwork, a positive attitude, adaptability and responsibility. Employability skills mirror essential skills in terms of providing a foundation for a successful, long-term career.



Useful Skill Web Sites
www15.hrdc drhc.gc.ca
Human Resources and Skills Development Canada hosts this site focusing on essential skills as part of an initiative launched in 2003. Information about understanding essential skills, occupational profiles, and external resources are organized for students, educators, and employers.

www.towes.com
This Test of Workplace Essential Skills (TOWES) is designed to accurately measure the three essential skills that are needed for safe and productive employment: Reading Text, Document Use and Numeracy.

www.cbelearn.ca/career/AboutYou/industryskills
The career services section of the Calgary Board of Education offers an overview of industry-specific skills as they pertain to a variety of job sectors. Students can investigate the skills required for jobs in everything from agriculture to health care. The site also offers industry skills assessments.
