What are the pros and cons of graduating from college early? (3-year vs 4-year plan)

I graduated MIT with a Computer Science B.S. degree in 3 years, from 2011 to 2014, so I can speak from my own experience.

Pros of graduating early:

  1. One fewer year of tuition, one more year of income. The difference was pretty substantial considering, as of 2014, MIT tuition & expenses was ~$60k per year, whereas an entry level software engineering position in Bay Area averaged $8090k. Also, when I was deciding between staying a 4th year to get a masters degree vs accepting one of my full time offers, a recruiter told me point blank that I wouldnt receive a higher offer with a masters degree next year than I would now.
  2. One more year of industry experience. During my college years, I had enjoyed my internships and hackathons significantly more than doing research. I knew from the beginning that I didnt want to pursue a life in academia, so I felt like Id learn more from being in the industry for a year, then being in academia.
  3. Personally, I felt justified to take more risks. Because I was 1 year younger than the other new grads at work, I was mentally more open to exploring career opportunities. For example, a little more than a year after I started working as a product engineer full time, I decided to try joining the infrastructure team, even though I knew next to nothing about infrastructure. Being the extremely competitive person that I was/am, I justified to myself that I had an extra year to try out things before falling behind of my peers. Again, this was purely psychological and personal, but it was definitely a factor in my decision process. I wrote about this transition here: Angela Zhang ()'s answer to What is it like to transition from a product engineer to an infrastructure engineer?

Cons of graduating early:

  1. Missing out on higher level classes. There were a lot of classes at MIT that I wish I had taken but didnt get a chance to, like Operating Systems, Performance Engineering, Database Systems, etc. Ive picked up some stuff covered in those classes during my day-to-day job, but I still wish I had studied them more systematically.
  2. Rushing through some of the learning opportunities. I didnt decide to graduate early until my 3rd year of college. As a result, I rushed through a lot of the mandatory classes and did the bare minimum to get a good enough grade. Part of me wish I had spent more time to go above and beyond the bare minimum, and gotten more out of those classes. Another example of missed learning opportunities is one more summers worth of research or internship opportunities. Looking back, I would have loved to spent a summer working in an enterprise software startup or a big-three consulting firm, just to see how different companies do things differently.
  3. Missing out of college friendships and the senior year experience. Most of my close friends in college were in my original class. As a result of graduating and moving to the West Coast early, I didnt get to spend senior year with them, going to silly events like Booze Cruise and Senior Week and all the other quintessential senior year of college experience. Due to the physical distance, I also didnt get to talk to to my friends or see them during that year as often as I would have liked. Fortunately, a lot of them ended up moving to Bay Area later and we were able to pick up our friendships right where we left off.

For what its worth, the decision for me was less whether I wanted to graduate in 3 years or 4, but more whether I wanted to stay for a 4th year to get a masters degree, or go work in the industry. I ultimately decided to join Quora full time instead of getting a masters degree because I believed Quora would be the best place for me to learn and grow as an engineer, and joining Quora a year late would mean missing out on being part of a smaller, scrappier team.

Im happy I made the decision to graduate early because then I got the opportunity to meet and work with a lot of incredible people, experience some of the growing pains of a smaller company, and be a part of the team earlier. That said, realistically in the long run, none of these pros and cons matter that much. One year of work or school experience is pretty insignificant compared to the rest of ones life. I honestly dont think my life would be that much different had I chosen to stay and get a masters degree.

The decision of graduating in 3 or 4 years is inherently personal, so if you are in a situation where you are trying to decide this for yourself, my advice would be to just consider the opportunity costs and do whatever feels right. Either way youll have a great time.

The biggest pro is going to be financial.  You'll save however much money it would have cost to go to college that final year and you'll also be able to hit the job market a year early, perhaps getting a 1 year head start on paying off your student loans, building wealth, etc...

The biggest con is going to be social.  You will never EVER be able to make friends the way you did in college once you hit adult life.  Ever.  The ability to make friends is, by FAR, the single biggest part of college I miss.  So you may miss out on whatever social connections you might have made that 4th year.  You might meet your wife or best friend that year.

It's not that you can't make friends as a working adult, but it's harder.  Much harder.  I find that making trustworthy friends, particularly male friends, is considerably more difficult than finding a dating partner.  People get more set in their ways as they get older.  The spontaneity goes away - people get settled into families and kids and routines, saddled with responsibilities.  That dominates their lives for the next several decades.  Plus you just don't encounter people in your age cohort as much.  Sometimes you make a friend but they get busy with their lives and never call you back. 

You can still have fun as a working aged adult but it's never the same.  I didn't realize how much life would become about routines and work and kids when I left college.  In particular I didn't realize how difficult & rare making new friends would become -- I mean actual friends who are people who you can count on, who want to hang out with you more than once every six months.  I have a lot of acquaintances and good colleagues & co-workers but not many friends.    My best friends are people I met in high school & college.

With the emergence of Advance Placement courses and online summer courses, many US students now have the ability to finish the four year degree in three or three and one-half years.  When they ask me if they should do that, i usually center the discussion around several issues:

Financial.  For the student who is paying between $40,000 to $50,000 for a year of college, this ends up being an 80 to 90 thousand dollar decision as it not only cuts the costs of college but allows the student to get into their starter job.

Social.  For most students, the four years of college are among the best and most memorable of their lives.  I have had students return years later to regret the decision they made to truncate the whole experience.  Often what they cut out, for instance, is that international semester. When I made the decision, they tell me, I didn't realize that work is just work.

Maturity. I have had a number of students who graduate very early and then struggle to compete for good jobs, largely because they are emotionally and physically immature. One will see some of these students enter the Peace Corps or some domestic program just to get ready for the eventual corporate or public sector career. The more entrepreneurial students just jump into their careers, often with the assistance of a more mature mentor figure.

The correct answer to this question varies based on the individual decision-maker.
