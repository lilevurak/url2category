Allhands: How did life get so crazy? A guide to what went wrong

Joanna Allhands, The Republic | azcentral.com Published 5:09 p.m. MT March 26, 2017 | Updated 7:09 a.m. MT April 13, 2017

How did my life get so insane?

It's a question you've probably asked yourself after putting in a full day at work, rushing to pick up the kids, slapping takeout on the table and falling into a heap on the couch.

This is the curse of modern life. We're so busy that we don't take time to relax, renew, connect with others or even sit down and think. We're constantly tired and harried, generally dissatisfied with the pace of life and utterly clueless about how to get off the hamster wheel.

Some argue that this is all in our heads: They say we have unrealistic expectations for our downtime, so we feel like our lives are out of balance even if we have more leisure time than our parents and grandparents did.

That's probably true.

But there's also plenty of evidence that we really are cramming too much into the 24 hours we have in a day and much of it has to do with our jobs.

We work way too much

Full-time employees work an average of 47 hours a week, according to a 2014 Gallup survey, a figure that has held steady for the last decade and a half. Nearly one in five Americans works more than 60 hours a week.

Who's spending so much time on the clock? Well, historically, it was employees with lower incomes and skill levels. (Remember the adage that CEOs spend all their time playing golf?)

But that has changed. Many lower skilled, part-time workers have seen their hours cut as companies try to keep rising benefits costs in check, while higher skilled, salaried workers are shouldering longer work days. Those in management and highly competitive industries, such as law and engineering, now typically put in the most hours per week.

The recession may be partly to blame for the shift. Many workers report feeling like they have to put in more hours to prove their worth and protect their jobs from downsizing. Companies that cut jobs during the downturn also never filled many of them, shoving that workload and, therefore, more hours required to get the job done on the poor souls that remained.

(Story continues below graphic)

Technology has changed work

But economists say this trend was in the works long before that and it hasn't changed even as the economy has improved. Part of the explanation is that technology is fundamentally changing how, when and why we work. Automation and artificial intelligence are replacing jobs, even those of skilled workers, and companies in virtually every industry are struggling to make sense of these new realities.

MY TURN:Computers, not offshoring, will steal your job

The good news is this technological revolution has given many workers the flexibility to work outside the office. Laptops and smartphones have increased our productivity.

But research suggests they also have increased how much we work and, in some cases, our superiors' expectations for when we're available. Too many workers don't know when to call it a day. Phone calls, emails and other work tasks often encroach into nights, weekends and family gatherings.

In effect, some folks never really turn work "off."

The male-female divide

Other research has shown that men particularly upper-class white men tend to put in the most hours at work. And that takes a toll on dual-income families, where fathers are increasingly expected to help out at home. Dad's long hours put more pressure on mom to handle the cooking, cleaning, groceries and child care in addition to whatever her paying job demands.

That juggling act gets tiring, which may help explain why women's participation in the workforce peaked in 2000 and has been dropping ever since. One study found 86 percent of moms who left professional jobs felt forced out by long, inflexible hours. Moms with partners who worked long hours were even more likely to quit.

What the imbalance is doing to us

Research suggests working too much can degrade our mental and physical health.  (Photo: Getty Images/iStockphoto)

None of this is good for society, of course. A recent Australian study found that working more than 39 hours a week or 34 hours for women, because of extra child care and housework duties can degrade mental and physical health. A multinational survey also found that American parents aren't as happy as their European counterparts, and they're far less happy than Americans without kids.

Overworking also can hurt companies' bottom lines. Burned-out employees are generally not as productive as those who come to work eager and refreshed, and the churn of workers can rack up recruiting and training costs, not to mention lost opportunities when a departing employee's work falls on a colleague (or simply falls through the cracks).

One study albeit a controversial one estimated absenteeism, turnover and lost productivity cost businesses at least $150 billion a year.

Why aren't we fixing it?

So, if finding a better work-life balance is so important, why aren't more people doing it?

Well, the short answer is that there is no one solution, no magic bullet to put everyone's work obligations in balance with the rest of their life obligations. Balance is subjective, and the load each of us carries is different.

But, as we'll see in the next two segments, there are things we can do as individuals and things employers and government can do to help.
