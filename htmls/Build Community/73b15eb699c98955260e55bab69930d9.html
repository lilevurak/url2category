Locals Give Back To Others To Honor Daughter's Spirit

Lauren Avezzie died 10 years ago in a drowning accident while vacationing on the Caribbean island of Dominica. Her parents, Cindi and David Avezzie, started a charitable foundation in her honor and are hosting the 10th annual Lauren E. Avezzie Memorial Golf Tournament on Sept. 18.

Ten years ago, David and Cindi Avezzie had their lives changed when they received a phone call telling them that their daughter, 22-year-old Lauren Avezzie, had died in a drowning accident while vacationing with friends on the Caribbean island of Dominica.

They will honor her with the 10th annual Lauren E. Avezzie Memorial Golf Tournament on Sept. 18.

Avezzie, a Burlington native and Falls Church, Virginia resident, was a graduate of the Master's School in West Simsbury and of Pepperdine University, where she earned degrees in Spanish studies and international studies.

At the end of her vacation, Lauren was all set to head to Chile, where she would participate in the WorldTeach program with Harvard University at the University of Santiago.

But her life was cut short, and her parents and sister, Alison, had to find a way to grieve.

"It was a devastating jolt to get the phone call that Lauren had drowned," Cindi Avezzie said at their Avon home, where the couple moved shortly after their daughter's death.

"It was crippling for me," David Avezzie said. "I don't think it's anything I'll ever get over. I weep and I still have times when my heart is broken. It's a human thing. I miss our daughter."

They credit their faith and their relationship with God for getting them through their toughest times, but they said there's no getting over what happened.

"You can't get over it, but you can get through it," said Cindi, who is a reading teacher at Classical Magnet School in Hartford. "We ran to God for comfort, healing, and support."

She also started a peer-led support group for Connecticut moms who have also faced the death of a child, called Hearts That H.E.A.L. Those moms have been together for six years now.

"It's very supportive," Cindi said. "We really lovingly support each other. That helps a lot to get through it."

"We aren't the only person who has lost a child and we won't be the last," David said.

What has helped maybe the most in the last 10 years, the two said, is helping others through the Lauren E. Avezzie Charitable Foundation, which they established after her death with the help of John Carmon of the Carmon Funeral Home.

By raising money through three special events and by receiving donations, they've been able to give $265,000 to area charities and organizations and hand out three annual scholarships in their daughter's name and spirit.

Her spirit, they said, was one of giving back to others. She participated in programs with her parents, including the Hoop It Up basketball program in Hartford with her mom and the Tumble Bunnies and Fit Kid Fun Camp programs with her father, who was a Farmington physical education teacher for 44 years.

"She was very good at reaching out to people in need," Cindi said. "To keep her spirit alive, we want to continue giving to people in need in her memory. That's keeping her spirit alive to us because she had the spirit of giving."

Among the beneficiaries of the Avezzie's foundation are the Connecticut Food Bank, the Farmington youth baseball league, Love146 in New Haven, Roaring Brook Nature Center in Canton, and Mary's Place in Windsor.

"There has been an outpouring of love, support and kindness, especially from the Farmington and Avon communities," Cindi said. "So we're always trying to give back to the community. It's so wonderful seeing lives being touched."

Lauren, who was an excellent Spanish speaker, was also passionate about understanding other cultures. It explains her love of travel and the time she spent in countries such as Argentina, Uruguay, and Brazil. She also worked at the embassy of Spain in Washington, D.C. to assist the ministry of education in facilitating culture and language.

"She was a person that was fascinated by people from other cultural groups," Cindi said. "She loved people of different cultures. She was fascinated by life."

Many of the people she met along her travels - some friends and others she was simply kind to - have stayed in touch with the Avezzie family, which means so much to her parents.

"These people were parts of Lauren and they help us stay connected to her," Cindi said.

Those interested in registering in or sponsoring the golf tournament, which starts at noon at the Farmington Woods golf course, can contact the Avezzie family at davidavezzie@comcast.net.
